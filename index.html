The Red Planet

Mars, the fourth planet from the Sun, has fascinated humans for centuries. With its reddish appearance, polar ice caps, and potential for past life, it remains a primary target for exploration.

Mars as seen from the Hubble Space Telescope. Credit: NASA/ESA

Planet Overview

Position & Orbit

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun, orbiting at an average distance of 227.9 million km (1.52 AU). A Martian year lasts 687 Earth days.

Mars has a thin atmosphere composed primarily of carbon dioxide (95%), with traces of nitrogen and argon.

Surface & Geology

Mars features the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and a gigantic canyon system, Valles Marineris, which is 10 times longer and 5 times deeper than the Grand Canyon.

The reddish color comes from iron oxide (rust) in the soil. The surface is rocky with evidence of ancient river valleys and lakebeds.

Climate & Conditions

Mars is a cold desert world with temperatures ranging from -125°C (-195°F) at the poles in winter to 20°C (70°F) at the equator during midday.

The planet has seasons like Earth due to its axial tilt, and polar ice caps made of water ice and frozen carbon dioxide.

Mars Exploration

Past Missions

Since the 1960s, numerous spacecraft have been sent to Mars including Mariner, Viking, Pathfinder, and Spirit/Opportunity rovers.

The Viking landers in 1976 were the first to successfully land and operate on Mars, conducting experiments to search for life.

Current Missions

NASA's Curiosity rover (landed 2012) and Perseverance rover (landed 2021) are currently exploring Mars, searching for signs of past life and collecting samples.

The Ingenuity helicopter made history as the first aircraft to fly on another planet in 2021.

Future Missions

NASA plans to return Martian samples to Earth in the 2030s through the Mars Sample Return mission.

SpaceX aims to send humans to Mars as early as the 2030s, with the ultimate goal of establishing a permanent human settlement.
